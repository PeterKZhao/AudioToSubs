Hayek, an Austrian economist and 1974 Nobel laureate in economics, systematically answered this question in the 20th century. His core contribution to the critique of modernity is a seemingly simple yet profoundly influential insight: the problem of knowledge. In 1945, Hayek published a paper, "The Application of Knowledge in Society," a work of less than 20 pages that fundamentally changed our understanding of planned economies. He posed the question:
Why is central planning impossible? Note that it's not about
difficulty or inefficiency, but about impossibility. This is a fundamental epistemological obstacle. Traditional critics of socialism would say that planned economies fail because of bureaucratic corruption, insufficient incentives, and abuse of power.
Hayek argued that even if all these problems were solved, even if you had a group of completely honest and diligent planners, planned economies would still fail. Why? Because knowledge is dispersed. Countless pieces of knowledge

He knows the soil characteristics of this hillside, and he knows which plots of land are suitable for which varieties. Knowing there's been more rain this year, how to adjust the harvest time, knowing which month's tea is of the best quality—this knowledge is specific, accumulated from 30 years of experience. Can you write this knowledge into a
manual? You
can, but you can never fully convey it because it involves too many subtle judgments, too many unspoken secrets.
This is what Michael Polanyi calls tacit knowledge—that is, we know far more than we can articulate. Now the question arises: how can an
agricultural bureaucrat sitting in Beijing grasp the
tea-growing knowledge of a certain mountain village in Yunnan?
Impossible. But if he needs to formulate a national agricultural plan, he must make decisions. What can he do? He can only simplify, using statistical data, averages, and standardized indicators. Yunnan Province has 1 million mu of tea plantations, with an estimated yield of 100,000 tons. This
figure seems scientific, but in reality, it erases all local differences and all contextual knowledge. The result? The plan is out of touch with reality, resource allocation is inefficient, and ultimately the entire system malfunctions. So how does the market solve this problem? The answer is the price mechanism. Price is a signal system; it compresses scattered knowledge into a number. Suppose a certain

Prices rise, and tea merchants will adjust their purchasing plans. Consumers will reduce their purchases or seek alternatives; farmers who grow other crops will consider switching to tea. The entire adjustment process requires no central planning; everyone simply needs to make decisions based on price signals that suit their own circumstances.
This is what Hayek called spontaneous order—the result of human action, but not a product of human design.
Markets are spontaneous orders; no one designed them, yet they function well. Languages ​​are spontaneous orders; no one designed English or Chinese, yet
they have evolved complex grammars and rich expressive capabilities. Common law is a spontaneous order; cases accumulate to form a legal system. These spontaneous orders share a common characteristic: they can utilize dispersed
knowledge, adapt to changing circumstances, and achieve coordination without central control. In contrast, rationally designed orders, such as planned economies, Esperanto, and codified laws, often function poorly because they cannot utilize local knowledge or adapt to complex situations. In his 1973 book, *Law, Legislation, and Liberty*, Hayek proposed a key concept:
constructive rationalism. This is the core ailment of modernity. What is constructive rationalism?
It is the belief that human reason can design everything and construct society from scratch. The philosophical origins of

Those spontaneously evolved institutions, customs, and rules contain the trial-and-error experience of several generations over hundreds of years.
You use
rational standards to examine them, find them unreasonable, and then try to replace them with a better design. But your "

transcended left and right. The Soviet planned economy was High Modernism; the American urban redevelopment movement was High Modernism; Le Corbusier's Radiant City design was High Modernism; and Robert Moses's transformation of New York City was also High Modernism. The allure of modernism lies in its promise to end chaos and disorder with

better world. The technological advancements of the 20th century—electricity, steel, concrete, automobiles—seemed to prove this promise achievable.
Scott's second
key concept is readability. For a state to manage society, it needs to understand it. However, traditional societies were

Thus, modern states initiated a series of readability projects: standardizing surnames (previously many only had given names), standardizing units of measurement, land surveying and registration, censuses and household registration systems, and grid-based urban planning. On the surface, these were all advancements; they indeed improved administrative efficiency. However, the problem is that in the pursuit of readability, those unreadable but crucial elements were often
destroyed. Let me give you a

A mixture of various trees: oak, beech, maple, spruce, varying in height and age. The understory

Those chaotic traditional forests had complex ecosystems. Different tree species were interdependent; understory plants protected the soil; dead branches and leaves provided nutrients; and birds controlled pests. This was a balanced system that had evolved over thousands of years.
Monoculture

But it is precisely this complexity that maintains the health of the forest. Let's look at a

This was not a natural disaster, but a

and ineffable knowledge, such as an old farmer's judgment in a specific situation: whether to water more this year, or whether this land is suitable for planting corn.
A core assumption of modernity is that Techne can replace Metis; as long as we scientize and standardize knowledge, we no longer need those empiricist elements. Scott argues that this is a fundamental mistake. It's not that Techne is unimportant, but rather that it cannot replace Metis. An agricultural
PhD might know all the theories of soil science, but if he hasn't actually farmed, his knowledge is
abstract.
A farmer who has farmed for 30 years, while not understanding chemical equations, possesses the wisdom to handle specific situations. The best scenario is a combination of both: Techne provides general principles, while Metis allows for flexible
application in specific contexts. The worst scenario is using Techne to suppress Metis, with experts sitting
in offices directing thousands of specific situations using standardized procedures.

The city is a complex self-organizing system, not a machine that can be designed from scratch. Those seemingly chaotic traditional neighborhoods, winding streets, mixed old and

Let's see how Taleb synthesizes this tradition and makes original contributions. What did Taleb inherit from
Burke? First, the Lindy effect: the longer something exists, the longer its expected remaining lifespan. Second, respect for tradition: time is the most rigorous selection mechanism.
Third, gradualism: small improvements are better than large revolutions.
From Hayek, Taleb inherited the limitations of knowledge: we know far less than we think. He inherited the idea that spontaneous order is superior to rational design: market language and common law are evolutionary. He also inherited opposition to centralization: centralized power equals the risk of single-point failure. From Scott, Taleb learned about Metis versus

Jacobs all presented qualitative analyses—insights, case studies, and arguments—Taleb reformulated these insights using mathematical language. For example, what is fragility? It's not a vague concept, but rather mathematical concavity: when volatility increases, your losses increase more than when volatility decreases. What is antifragility? It's convexity: the ability to benefit from volatility. The greater the
volatility, the greater your gains. What are the benefits of this mathematization? It allows intuition to be precisely expressed, arguments to be rigorously proven, and strategies to be quantified and implemented. Second, there's the
concept of "skin in the game," Taleb's core original concept. Scott distinguished between Metis and Techne, but his primary focus was on the nature of knowledge. Taleb asks who bears the consequences. Why
is Metis reliable?
Not because it's more scientific, but because those who possess Metis must bear the consequences.
Farmers who plant wrong crops starve; captains who make wrong judgments sink. Why is Techne dangerous?
Not because it's inaccurate,
but because those who possess Techne often don't bear the consequences.
Economists who predict wrong continue to collect consulting fees. When a city planner's design fails, they can try designing another
city. This transforms an epistemological problem into an
ethical one: who has the right to make decisions? The answer is those who bear the consequences. The third original contribution is the concept of
antifragility itself. This transcends traditional robustness. Traditional risk management aims for strength—the ability to withstand shocks
and remain unchanged, like building a sufficiently strong bridge to withstand a once-in-a-century flood. Taleb argues that this is insufficient; better is antifragility—not only able to withstand shocks but also to benefit from them. For example, the human immune system doesn't maintain health by isolating itself from all pathogens, but by developing
antibodies through contact with a small number of pathogens, becoming stronger. A completely sterile environment actually weakens the immune system. This insight is missing in traditional conservative thought. Burke and Hayek emphasize non-destruction, a defensive approach. Taleb points out an offensive strategy: proactively seeking moderate pressure and benefiting from

Taleb is

Throughout the 20th century, industrial society pursued the same goal: eliminating redundancy and increasing efficiency. But is redundancy really waste? Further, to reduce costs, the supply chain became globalized: chips were manufactured in Taiwan, assembled in China, and parts were imported from Japan, South Korea, Germany, and the United States.
This leveraged the comparative

For example, only TSMC could produce certain special chips, and the main production area for certain rare earth materials was in China. This achieved economies of scale, reducing costs. The

n't buy chips and had to reduce production. Consumers wanted to buy cars, but… 4S stores have no cars in stock, and you have to wait several months. In 2022,

financial indicators as the sole objective. This is the distortion of manufacturing caused by excessive
financialization. A similar logic is even more evident in the financial sector. During the 1990s and 2000s, when Greenspan was Chairman of the Federal Reserve, his policy philosophy was to eliminate small fluctuations and pursue

subprime
mortgage crisis nearly caused the global
financial system to collapse. This wasn't a black swan event, but the inevitable result of over a decade of policies aimed at eliminating volatility. Planners, in order to control the system,
need to make it readable, simplified, standardized, and noise-eliminating. However, the resilience of complex systems comes precisely from those seemingly chaotic diversityes and seemingly wasteful redundancies. If you eliminate them all for optimization, it may seem more efficient under normal circumstances,
but the system becomes fragile.
Once a shock occurs, the entire system collapses. Extreme specialization
equals single-point failure risk. Another core tenet of modernity is that the more specialized the division of labor, the higher the efficiency. Adam Smith, in the opening of *The Wealth of
Nations*, uses the example
of needle makers to illustrate the benefits of specialization. One worker completes all the processes, making 20 needles a day. After specialization, 10 workers make 48,000 needles a day, increasing
efficiency by 240 times. This is indeed the foundation of modern industry. Without specialization, there would be no Industrial Revolution, and no current level of productivity. However, modern society has pushed the division of labor to
extremes. It's no longer about being a doctor, but an orthopedic surgeon specializing in left knee cartilage degeneration; no longer about being an engineer,
