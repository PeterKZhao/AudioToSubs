Historical comparisons show the impact on Europe. In contrast to East Asia, where Europe remained unified for
centuries over the past two millennia, with hundreds of small states,
city-states, and principalities competing, learning through trial and error, and giving rise to modern science,
democracy, and the
Industrial Revolution, East Asia pursued unification. The Qin Dynasty's prefecture-county system and centralized power led to overexpansion, collapse, fragmentation, and reunification—a cycle repeated for 2,000 years.
Why was Europe more successful?
Because decentralization allowed for trial and error. One country's failure didn't affect others; one country innovated,
and others learned. With unification, the emperor's mistakes were implemented nationwide; errors resulted in national catastrophe. Talebhayek advocated decentralization, local autonomy, and federalism over centralization; small states were superior to large states, and city-states to empires. Liu Zhongjing's theory of the various Xia states is a right-wing critique of modernity applied in the Chinese context. A unified empire is fragile; a collapse means total
collapse. The small states of the various Xia states are anti-fragile, diversifying risk
and allowing for individual trial and error.
You may disagree with his political views, but theoretically, a fragmented system of small states is indeed more stable than unification. However, in reality, the system is difficult to change in the short
term. Why? Because the current system favors the elite, who reap upward
benefits. The risk is being shifted to you.
Why would they want to change? The inertia of the system is enormous. There are too-to-fail banks, a powerful central government, and a financialized economy—all of which have huge vested
interest groups. So what can you do? It depends on where you are. If you are
in the free world, then congratulations, you have the potential to drive change. But don't misunderstand, I'm not saying you have to convince the majority, or that you have to wait for
51% to agree before you can change anything. In *Asymmetric Risk*, Taleb offers a counterintuitive insight: changing the world doesn't require a majority, only a
determined minority. He cites Kosher as an example. American Jews make up only 2% of the population, and those who strictly adhere to Kosher laws are even fewer. But if you go to the supermarket, you'll see many food products with Kosher certification.
Why?
Because Jews are an uncompromising minority; they absolutely refuse to eat non-Kosher food.
