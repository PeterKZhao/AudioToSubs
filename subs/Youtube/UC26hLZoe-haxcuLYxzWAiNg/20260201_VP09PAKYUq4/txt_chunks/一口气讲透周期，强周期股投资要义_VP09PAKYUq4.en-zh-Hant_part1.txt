Today, we'll thoroughly discuss the concept of cycles.
Many people find cycles mysterious,
like some profound economic theory,
but it's actually very simple.
There's a very simple model in physics
: a pendulum. If
you pull it to one side and release it, it will swing back and
forth. Why? Because two factors are at play: gravity pulls it towards its equilibrium position—this is the constraint force. But a moving object has the tendency to continue moving—this is inertia. When you pull the pendulum away, gravity pulls it back. In the process of pulling it back, it gains velocity. When it reaches the
equilibrium position, the effect of gravity cancels out,
but the velocity remains,
so it will rush past the equilibrium point. After rushing past,
gravity acts in the opposite direction, pulling it back again. This cycle repeats, and that's the cycle. The essence of all cycles is this mechanism: constraint
plus inertia.
Why do we have the four seasons of spring, summer,
autumn, and
winter? Because the Earth revolves around the Sun, the Sun's gravity is a constraint, and the Earth's speed is inertia. If there were only gravity and no inertia, the Earth would fall directly into the Sun; if there were only
inertia and no gravity, the Earth would fly away. The balance between constraint and inertia results in periodic orbital motion. Combined with the Earth's axial tilt, the point of direct sunlight oscillates between the Tropic of Cancer and the Tropic of Capricorn throughout the year, thus forming the four seasons. The cycles of nature are essentially constrained movements created by certain conservation laws and boundary conditions. The same principle applies to economic and social cycles. What are the constraints? Limited
resources, limited production capacity,
limited credit,
limited market capacity. What is the inertia? The inertia of human expectations, the momentum of investment, and the inertia of institutional
mechanisms. When the economy is good, people believe it will continue to be good—this is the inertia of expectations. Entrepreneurs believe demand will continue to grow, so they invest heavily; banks believe loans are safe, so they lend heavily; consumers believe income will continue to rise, so they consume heavily,
even borrowing money to consume. Everyone's behavior reinforces this trend, thus forming a positive
feedback loop. But
can this loop continue indefinitely? No, because there are constraints, such as limited
resources like iron ore, copper ore, and oil. Reserves are finite, extraction speed has an upper limit, and production capacity is also limited. Factory equipment and
labor can't be added at will, and credit is finite; people can't borrow indefinitely. When investment exceeds resource constraints, it overheats, inflation rises, raw material prices soar, wages increase, and
all costs rise. Businesses find they can't
make money and
begin to contract.
During contraction,
everyone thinks things will continue to worsen, so
entrepreneurs frantically cut investment,
banks frantically collect loans, and
consumers frantically save money.
Everyone's behavior reinforces this trend, creating a
negative feedback loop.
But when contraction reaches a certain point,
assets become cheaper,
opportunities arise, and
people dare to invest again,
starting a new cycle. In short, it's
an over-correction cycle. It
overshoots during rises and overshoots during falls.
Why does it overshoot?
Because people's expectations have inertia, and
adjustments are delayed.
In mathematical terms,
it's the oscillating solution of a second-order differential equation.
Any system whose
rate of change
depends on its deviation from equilibrium has an
oscillating solution. This applies to a simple
pendulum and the
economy.
Philosophically speaking,
cycles are the movement of contradictions.
Hegel spoke of the negation of negation, and the

transformation is not instantaneous
but rather a spiraling, cyclical process.
Because systems have inertia, delays, and memories,
cycles are not mysterious;
they are a
common characteristic of any constrained dynamic system. Everything has its time—
this is the theoretical basis of cycles.
Economists have studied this for over a century and have
indeed discovered some patterns.
They have
discovered cycles of different lengths from different perspectives.
Let me systematically explain it to you. The
Kitchin cycle, 3-4 years, is an inventory cycle.
Discovered in 1932 by the British economist Kitchin,
he studied economic data from Britain and the United States and
found that there is roughly a small cycle every 3-4 years.
How does this cycle occur? It's the
time lag in companies adjusting their inventory. When
companies see good demand and
sales growth, they
feel the need to increase inventory. Increasing inventory requires
production, which in turn requires purchasing raw materials and
hiring workers.
This process itself creates new demand.
Upstream suppliers receive more orders,
workers receive wages, and
can consume, leading to a
better economy.
But once the inventory is sufficient,
companies find that they can't sell their goods;
demand suddenly disappears.
Why?
Because some of the demand in the previous period
was for replenishing inventory,
not genuine end-consumer demand.
Now that the inventory is replenished... When the demand for restocking disappears,
aggregate demand decreases, forcing
companies to reduce inventory. This leads to production cuts, which in turn leads to layoffs.
Reduced orders from upstream suppliers result in
unemployment, decreased
consumption, and a weakening
economy. Once inventory levels
reach a certain point and become
very low,
companies find that even a slight increase in demand is
insufficient, necessitating inventory replenishment and the
start of a new cycle.
This cycle typically lasts 3-4 years
because the time lag between companies assessing demand, replenishing inventory, and reducing inventory
adds up to
roughly this timeframe. The constraints are the company's cash
flow capacity and warehousing costs. Inventory
cannot be increased indefinitely; cash needs to circulate, warehouses incur rent, and there are losses. Replenishment
reaches a certain level, increasing
financial pressure and necessitating a halt. The Juglar cycle, a 7-11 year equipment investment cycle, was discovered by the
French economist Juglar in 1862, more than 60 years before Kitchin. He studied economic data from France, Britain, and the United
States and found a cycle
roughly every 7-11 years. This cycle arises from fixed asset investment. When companies see a favorable industry with high profit margins, they expand production capacity.
Expanding production capacity requires investment in new equipment and factories. Investment itself creates demand; equipment manufacturers get more orders, construction workers'
wages increase, and the economy prospers. However, once all the equipment is invested in and the factories are built, investment demand
disappears.
Equipment manufacturers lose their orders,
construction workers lose their jobs, and the
economy declines. Then, a new round of investment demand only arises when the equipment has depreciated, the
technology becomes obsolete,
or it breaks down. Why is this cycle 7-11 years? Because the average lifespan of industrial equipment is this long. Machines need to be replaced after seven or eight years, but companies certainly won't replace them every day; they will concentrate on replacing them at a certain time.
What are the constraints? The
lifespan of fixed assets and the company's investment capacity. Equipment
cannot break down immediately; under normal circumstances, it can be used for seven or eight to ten years. Companies also have limited funds; they
cannot invest on a large scale every year. Investments have
cycles. They dare to invest only when they make money; they
dare not invest when they lose money. The Kuznets cycle, a
15-25 year construction cycle, was discovered by
Kuznets in 1930 when studying the US economy. It is also called the housing cycle. He was the 1971 Nobel Prize laureate in
economics and a master of studying long-term economic growth. How does this cycle occur? The real estate investment cycle is longer because houses are used for longer periods; a single building can last for decades or even centuries. Therefore, the investment
cycle in the construction industry is also
longer. Furthermore, real estate investment is closely related to population cycles. When there are many young people buying houses, the demand for marriage is high, leading to robust real estate investment. Once this generation has bought their homes, the next generation hasn't yet grown up, causing real estate investment to shrink. What are the constraints? Population growth, urbanization,
and land supply. When urbanization is rapid, a large influx of
people from rural areas into cities creates
strong demand for
housing. When urbanization slows down, those who should have moved to the city have already moved, leading to a shrinking demand for housing.
Population structure is also a constraint. When there are many
young people, the proportion of the 20-35 age group is high, resulting in strong demand for housing. As the population ages, the proportion of this age group declines,
weakening demand for housing.
Land is also a constraint. There's only so much land in the city center; once developed, it's gone. Expansion must either extend to the suburbs or increase the floor area ratio. However, there are limits to suburban expansion and floor area ratio increases. The Kondratiev cycle, a
50-60 year innovation cycle, is the longest cycle. This is what we often call the Kondratiev wave, proposed by the Soviet economist Kondratiev in 1926. He studied economic data from Britain, France, the United States, and Germany from 1780 to
1920 and found that there was a major cycle roughly every 50 to 60 years. Unfortunately, after proposing this
theory, he was arrested by the Soviet government on charges of counter-revolution and executed in 1938. The charges were that he promoted the theory of capitalist cycles and undermined the legitimacy of the Soviet planned economy. However, his theory has survived and been verified countless times. He
discovered that each major cycle corresponds to a technological revolution: the first from 1780 to 1850 – steam engines and textiles; the second from 1850 to 1900 – railways and steel; the third from 1900 to 1950 –
electricity and chemicals; the fourth from
1950 to 2000 – automobiles and electronics; and the fifth from 2000 to the present – ​​information technology and the internet. Schumpeter later further developed this theory, proposing the concept of creative destruction. He said that the essence of capitalism is creative destruction; with the emergence of new technologies, old
industries are eliminated. Horse-drawn carriages were replaced by automobiles, gasoline lamps by
electric lights, telegraphs by telephones, and
telephones by
the internet. Each technological revolution brings decades of
prosperity. However, when this technological paradigm reaches its limit, growth stagnates, and a new cycle begins only after the next
revolutionary
technology emerges. What are the constraints? The limits of technological paradigm diffusion. No matter how much you improve the steam engine, its efficiency is limited. No matter how many railways are built, there are only
a few main lines. No matter how widespread automobiles become, a family will only have two or three. No matter how much the internet is promoted, the number of users is only a few billion globally. Every
technological paradigm has its ceiling. When penetration approaches 100%, there is no more incremental
growth, only replacement of existing stock. At this point, economic growth slows down. What happens then? Overinvestment, overcapacity, price crashes, bankruptcies, financial crises, and a prolonged economic recession. Economic prosperity only resumes when the next
revolutionary technology emerges and
new investment opportunities arise. However, technological breakthroughs
are accidental;
you
can't have
them just because you want them. It might take 10 years, or it might take 20 years.
This is the recession phase of the Kondratiev
wave. Let's use an abstract, nested structure to explain how the complexity of real-world cycles occurs. This nested structure has three layers. The first layer, at its core, is the cycle of human fear and greed. Traditional
economics assumes that people are
rational,
but in reality, people are fundamentally irrational. Imagine a perfect world with unlimited resources, unchanged credit, unchanged policies, and unchanged technology.
Would there still be cycles? Of course, because people experience fear and greed. The stock market is the best example. The same company, with unchanged fundamentals and profits, can see its stock price rise from $10 to $100, then fall back to $10. Why? Because the
market is pricing in expectations, and expectations are self-reinforcing. When prices rise, people think they will rise further, so they buy, pushing up prices. The rising prices validate the expectations, making them even stronger. The media also fuels the fire, and FOMO (Fear of Missing Out) begins to spread. This is positive feedback.
However, positive feedback loops have limits. Once
prices rise to a certain level, the rate of new capital
entering the market can no longer keep up with the greed of existing capital, so a reversal begins. As soon as selling begins, the upward trend stops.
Those who chased the high panic and start selling. The more people sell, the faster prices fall, and the
greater the panic. Stop-loss orders are triggered, leverage is liquidated, and a stampede
begins. This
cycle is entirely a pendulum swing of human nature, unrelated to external constraints. Keynes's beauty contest theory illustrates this point: you're not choosing the most beautiful person, but rather guessing who others will choose. This game generates spontaneous fluctuations, even if beauty itself is constant. Soros's theory of reflexivity goes a step further: participants' perceptions influence reality, and reality, in turn, influences perceptions. A rising stock price makes it easier for a
company to raise funds, which allows for expansion. Expansion leads to better performance, which in turn drives up the stock price—a positive feedback loop. The reverse is also true. Therefore, financial markets are inherently unstable, naturally prone to bubbles and crashes. The second
layer, the intermediate layer, involves the cyclical changes of specific constraints. In the real world, constraints are
not constant
but cyclical.
These changes don't arise out of thin air but are driven by larger, higher-level constraints, such as the credit cycle. Why does the
government sometimes loosen credit and sometimes tighten it? Because there's a larger constraint behind it: fiscal sustainability. When the economy is weak, the government
stimulates growth by loosening credit, lowering interest rates, and expanding the fiscal deficit. This stimulates the
economy, but at the cost of rising debt and inflation. When the debt ratio becomes too high or inflation becomes too severe, fiscal constraints come into play. A high debt ratio
means… A large portion of fiscal revenue goes towards debt repayment, leaving no money for construction. Inflation becomes too high, unbearable for ordinary people, leading to social instability. The
government realizes that if this continues, fiscal collapse and social instability will ensue, so it must tighten credit, raise interest
rates, and
reduce the fiscal deficit.
This controls
debt and inflation, but at the cost of economic recession. Businesses can't borrow money, investment decreases, employment decreases, and consumption decreases, creating a vicious cycle. When the economic recession reaches a certain point,
unemployment becomes too high, businesses collapse en
masse, and
social instability returns.
The government can't withstand the pressure, so it has to loosen restrictions again. This is a nested hierarchy of constraints: economic growth requires credit;
credit
expansion is constrained by inflation and debt; inflation and debt are constrained by fiscal sustainability and social stability. Each cycle is driven by the constraints of the layer above. The third and outermost layer, the fundamental constraint, is a slow-moving variable:
fiscal sustainability. What constrains it? It's the political system. In democratic countries, the election cycle is four years. Politicians
wanting re-election must produce results during their term. How to achieve results quickly? Monetary stimulus,
lowering
unemployment, increasing GDP, and boosting the stock market, making voters
happy. As for the risk of inflation
due to debt accumulation, that's a matter for the next administration. China's situation is slightly different. China doesn't have an electoral system, but it has another constraint: growth targets. To maintain growth, local governments rely on land revenue and urban investment bonds, leading to excessively high debt levels. The
central
government then has to deleverage. However, once deleveraging reaches a certain point,
economic
growth slows, employment pressure increases, and the central government may have to loosen monetary policy again to stabilize growth. The 2020 pandemic led to further monetary easing, and in 2021, growth picked up,
so tightening began again.
This is why
China's credit cycle is particularly pronounced and shorter than that of the US. Behind each tightening or loosening is a struggle between growth targets and debt sustainability. Looking further ahead, there
are also constraints from technological

Fiscal sustainability is constrained by

Many say that food is the most important thing for people, and consumption is an eternal need, so how can it have cycles? There are too many constraints on consumption. The first constraint is income. During periods of rapid economic growth in developed countries, consumption appears robust. Wages

With children and increased independence, consumer desires have declined significantly. Developed countries are generally entering an aging society, meaning a necessary shift in consumption patterns.
